# 2. Related Work

## 2.1 Breast Ultrasound Image Segmentation

Automated segmentation of breast lesions in ultrasound imaging has been investigated for over two decades, driven by the clinical need for consistent, observer-independent lesion delineation. Early work relied on active contour models, graph cuts, and level set methods, each requiring substantial hand-crafted feature engineering and domain-specific parameter tuning. Xian et al. (2018) provided a comprehensive survey spanning these classical approaches and the transition toward learning-based methods, identifying boundary ambiguity, acoustic shadowing, and heterogeneous lesion appearance as the core challenges distinguishing breast ultrasound segmentation from natural image tasks.

The introduction of deep convolutional networks substantially advanced the field. Yap et al. (2018) conducted one of the earliest systematic comparisons of deep learning methods for breast ultrasound lesion detection and segmentation, establishing benchmark baselines and highlighting the difficulty of generalizing across different scanner types and acquisition protocols. Gu et al. (2020) proposed CA-Net, a comprehensive attention convolutional network combining channel, spatial, and scale attention mechanisms to handle multi-scale lesion variability, demonstrating that attention-guided feature selection produces more discriminative segmentation boundaries than standard encoder-decoder designs.

More recently, transformer-based architectures have entered the breast ultrasound segmentation domain. Chen et al. (2021) demonstrated that hybrid CNN-transformer designs — combining local convolutional feature extraction with global self-attention — outperform purely convolutional models on several medical segmentation benchmarks, establishing the encoder-decoder transformer paradigm. The introduction of the Segment Anything Model (SAM) by Kirillov et al. (2023) prompted extensive exploration of foundation model adaptation for medical imaging. Ma et al. (2024) developed MedSAM, a fine-tuned version of SAM on over one million medical image-mask pairs, demonstrating broad generalization across modalities. Domain-specific adaptations for breast ultrasound followed: Li et al. (2025) proposed a dual-branch SAM-transformer fusion network that combines the boundary sensitivity of SAM with the local texture representation of a Swin Transformer, achieving state-of-the-art results on the BUSI dataset.

Despite these architectural advances, benchmark comparisons in breast ultrasound segmentation remain fragmented. Thomas et al. (2023) introduced BUS-Set, a fully reproducible benchmark aggregating 1,154 images from four public datasets, identifying significant variation in reported Dice scores across studies that use different data splits, preprocessing choices, and evaluation implementations — even when applying the same architectures. This fragmentation is compounded by a less-discussed factor: the proportion of normal (lesion-free) cases in the evaluation set varies substantially across datasets, and its effect on reported metric values is rarely controlled or reported. The present work directly addresses this gap.

## 2.2 Deep Learning Architectures for Medical Image Segmentation

The encoder-decoder architecture with skip connections, introduced by Ronneberger et al. (2015) as U-Net, remains the foundational design for medical image segmentation. Its ability to preserve spatial detail through skip connections while learning hierarchical features through contracting and expanding paths has made it the most widely adapted architecture across imaging modalities. The substitution of task-specific encoders with pretrained convolutional backbones — particularly ResNet variants (He et al., 2016) — has further improved performance by leveraging ImageNet transfer learning without increasing inference complexity.

A systematic review by Siddique et al. (2021) catalogued more than 30 U-Net variants, grouping them by their modifications to skip connections, attention mechanisms, and encoder design. Among the most influential variants are Attention U-Net (Oktay et al., 2018), which uses squeeze-and-excitation attention gates to suppress non-target activations at each skip connection; U-Net++ (Zhou et al., 2019), which introduces densely nested sub-encoders between each encoder and decoder level to reduce the semantic gap at skip connections; MA-Net (Fan et al., 2020), which incorporates multi-scale channel attention at both the encoder and decoder sides; and LinkNet (Chaurasia and Culurciello, 2017), which shares encoder weights with the decoder by adding them directly rather than concatenating, yielding a lightweight design suited to resource-constrained settings.

Transformer-based models have increasingly challenged CNN dominance. TransUNet (Chen et al., 2021) embeds a Vision Transformer into the encoder bottleneck of U-Net, capturing long-range dependencies that convolutional kernels cannot model efficiently. Swin-UNet (Cao et al., 2023) replaced all convolutional components with hierarchical Swin Transformer blocks, achieving strong performance on abdominal organ segmentation. Foundation model adaptations such as MedSAM (Ma et al., 2024) represent a paradigm shift toward prompt-driven, general-purpose segmentation, though their performance on lesion-level tasks with high boundary ambiguity — such as breast ultrasound — remains dependent on prompt quality and fine-tuning strategy. The five architectures evaluated in the present work represent the established CNN encoder-decoder family, chosen specifically to isolate the effect of evaluation protocol differences from architectural confounds.

## 2.3 Evaluation Metrics and Reproducibility in Segmentation Benchmarks

The Dice similarity coefficient (Dice, 1945), equivalent to the F1 score applied pixel-wise, has become the primary evaluation metric for binary medical image segmentation due to its intuitive interpretation and robustness to foreground-background class imbalance (Taha and Hanbury, 2015). The Dice coefficient is typically combined with the Hausdorff distance as a complementary boundary accuracy measure, a pairing recommended by Taha and Hanbury (2015) as sufficient for characterizing volumetric segmentation quality. The Intersection over Union (IoU), also known as the Jaccard index, is mathematically related to Dice by IoU = Dice / (2 − Dice) and is often reported alongside it.

Despite its widespread adoption, the Dice coefficient has known limitations. Sudre et al. (2017) proposed the generalised Dice overlap to address class-frequency imbalance by weighting the contribution of each class inversely proportional to its occurrence volume, improving stability on highly imbalanced problems. Jadon (2020) surveyed a broader family of Dice-based loss functions, documenting numerical instabilities in low-prevalence settings. Bertels et al. (2019) analysed Dice as an optimization objective and noted that the standard smoothed formulation behaves anomalously on empty ground-truth regions, but did not systematically quantify this effect on dataset-level evaluation or model ranking.

Seghier (2024) identified a related set of methodological issues in Dice-based reporting, proposing ten rules for transparent calculation and interpretation. Among the anomalies identified is the paradoxical increase of the reported Dice score when images with no positive instances are added to the evaluation set — precisely the distortion mechanism analyzed and formalized in the present work. This finding independently confirms the existence of the empty-mask pathology from a different empirical angle, but without quantifying the threshold at which ranking inversion occurs or proposing an alternative evaluation protocol.

At a broader level, Reinke et al. (2024) and Maier-Hein et al. (2022) catalogued pitfalls across a wide range of image analysis metrics through the Metrics Reloaded initiative, recommending that researchers select metrics based on the clinical task structure rather than convention. Their framework acknowledges that a single aggregate score cannot simultaneously capture segmentation quality, detection sensitivity, and specificity — the exact conflation that motivates the Stratified Evaluation Framework (SEF) proposed in this paper.

Reproducibility remains a persistent challenge in medical image segmentation benchmarks. Thomas et al. (2023) demonstrated that Dice scores for identical architectures on the same dataset can vary by more than 10 percentage points depending on the split, preprocessing, and implementation — and crucially, that this variation exceeds the gap between the best and worst architectures in several comparisons. The present work identifies a further, previously uncharacterized source of such variation: the handling of normal cases with empty ground-truth masks, which interacts with model-specific false-positive rates to produce metric distortions that corrupt cross-model comparisons without any change to segmentation quality on positive cases.
